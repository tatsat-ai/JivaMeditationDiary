**Diary Entry:**
The first time. There was an unfamiliar quietness to the space before I even began. It felt like a vast, open field, waiting to be filled.

I started with the Pancha Tattva mantra. It was a simple anchor, a grounding sound that pulled my awareness out of the immediate clutter of the day and into a singular point of vibration. It felt like acknowledging a fundamental truth before diving into the deeper waters.

Then came the Mahamantra. The sheer volume of sound, the repetition, demanded physical presence. The initial rounds were a struggle—my mind was a restless current, eager to latch onto external concerns. I felt the usual internal friction, the urge to analyze the words rather than simply allow them to flow.

**Deep Insights and Realizations:**
The understanding—Hare is Srimati Radhika, Krishna is the attractor—was the hinge point. When I tried to hold that concept while the sounds were happening, the effort was immense. It wasn't a sudden flash of insight; it was more like holding a very delicate, vibrating thread.

**Effect of Finger Awareness Practice:**
The finger practice proved to be the most immediate tool. When I noticed my attention slipping—when the rhythm fractured and I started thinking about the task ahead, or replaying a conversation—I would gently bring my focus back to the physical sensation of the finger. It was a very small, sharp correction. It didn't stop the distraction entirely, but it created a feedback loop: *Distraction occurs -> I notice -> I return*. This process, repeated, felt like training a muscle, teaching the mind that returning to the sound, or to the simple act of awareness, was a viable option.

What arose wasn't a grand revelation, but a profound sense of *relinquishing control*. For the duration of the chanting, I was trying to *force* my attention onto the sound, and the effort was exhausting. When I finally allowed the chanting to simply *be*, without the pressure to achieve a specific state or understanding, the noise softened.

**Nature of Distracting Thoughts:**
The thoughts that pulled me away were predominantly logistical: planning, remembering things I needed to do, and judging the quality of my focus. They were insistent, demanding immediate attention.

**Experience as Jiva:**
The lasting impression is the texture of patience. The practice revealed that awareness isn't about eliminating thought; it’s about changing the relationship to it. The finger awareness wasn't about stopping the thoughts; it was about noticing the *act* of letting them go, even for a second, and returning to the sound. It felt less like achieving a destination and more like learning to navigate a turbulent sea. I left the session feeling tired, but strangely hollowed out, as if the constant internal striving had finally eased into a slow, steady rhythm.
